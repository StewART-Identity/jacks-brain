A reference overview of human brain anatomy and function, covering the major structural divisions, supporting tissues, blood supply, and the twelve cranial nerves. Written for a general medical audience.

## Composition

The brain weighs approximately 3 pounds in adults and is roughly 60% fat; the remainder is water, protein, carbohydrates, and salts. It is not a muscle — it contains blood vessels, neurons, and glial cells. Together with the spinal cord, it forms the [[collection/concepts/central-nervous-system]].

## Gray matter and white matter

[[collection/concepts/gray-matter-and-white-matter]] describes two distinct tissue types in the CNS:

- **Gray matter** — the darker outer portion of the brain (reversed in the spinal cord). Primarily composed of neuron somas (cell bodies). Responsible for processing and interpreting information.
- **White matter** — the lighter inner section. Primarily composed of axons wrapped in myelin (a protective sheath). Responsible for transmitting information to other parts of the nervous system.

## Main structural divisions

See [[collection/concepts/brain-anatomy]] for a detailed breakdown. At the highest level:

### Cerebrum

The largest part of the brain, comprising the cerebral cortex (gray matter) and white matter at its center. Initiates and coordinates movement, regulates temperature, enables speech, judgment, reasoning, problem-solving, emotion, and learning.

- **Cerebral cortex**: the outer gray layer, divided into two hemispheres connected by the corpus callosum. Covered with ridges (gyri) and folds (sulci). The right hemisphere controls the left side of the body and vice versa.
- **Lobes**: each hemisphere has four lobes — frontal (personality, decision-making, Broca's area for speech), parietal (spatial reasoning, pain, Wernicke's area for language comprehension), occipital (vision), and temporal (short-term memory, speech, musical rhythm).

### Brainstem

Connects the cerebrum to the spinal cord. Comprises:

- **Midbrain (mesencephalon)**: complex clusters of nuclei and neural pathways for hearing, movement, and response calculation. Contains the substantia nigra (dopamine-rich; affected in Parkinson's disease) and the basal ganglia.
- **Pons**: origin of four of the twelve cranial nerves; bridges the midbrain and medulla.
- **Medulla**: where brain meets spinal cord; regulates heart rhythm, breathing, blood flow, oxygen/CO₂ levels; produces reflexes (sneezing, vomiting, coughing, swallowing).

### Cerebellum

The "little brain" — fist-sized, at the back and below the temporal/occipital lobes. Coordinates voluntary muscle movement; maintains posture, balance, and equilibrium. Emerging research explores its roles in thought, emotion, social behavior, and involvement in addiction, autism, and schizophrenia.

## Brain coverings: meninges

Three protective layers surround the brain and spinal cord:

1. **Dura mater** (outermost): thick and tough; two sub-layers with passageways for veins and arteries supplying the brain.
2. **Arachnoid mater** (middle): thin, weblike, no nerves or blood vessels; CSF circulates below it.
3. **Pia mater** (innermost): thin membrane hugging the brain surface, rich in veins and arteries.

## Deep structures

- **Pituitary gland**: the "master gland"; regulates hormone flow from thyroid, adrenals, ovaries, and testicles. Receives chemical signals from the hypothalamus.
- **Hypothalamus**: above the pituitary; regulates body temperature, sleep, hunger, thirst, memory, and emotion.
- **Amygdala**: almond-shaped, one per hemisphere; regulates emotion and memory; associated with the reward system, stress, and fight-or-flight response.
- **Hippocampus**: seahorse-shaped, on the underside of each temporal lobe; supports memory, learning, navigation, and spatial perception; implicated in Alzheimer's disease.
- **Pineal gland**: responds to light/dark; secretes melatonin to regulate circadian rhythms and the sleep-wake cycle.
- **Ventricles**: four open chambers manufacturing cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which cushions the CNS, removes waste, and delivers nutrients.

## Blood supply

Two arterial systems supply the brain:

- **Carotid arteries**: external (pulse-point on the neck) and internal branches circulating blood to the front of the brain.
- **Vertebral arteries**: follow the spinal column; join at the brainstem to form the **basilar artery**, supplying the rear.
- **Circle of Willis**: a loop of vessels at the bottom of the brain connecting the two systems, allowing front-to-back circulation and arterial communication.

## Cranial nerves

Twelve nerves originate inside the cranium. The first two arise from the cerebrum; the remaining ten emerge from the brainstem:

| # | Name | Function |
|---|------|----------|
| 1 | Olfactory | Smell |
| 2 | Optic | Eyesight |
| 3 | Oculomotor | Pupil response, eye motion |
| 4 | Trochlear | Eye muscles |
| 5 | Trigeminal | Sensation (scalp, teeth, jaw, face); chewing |
| 6 | Abducens | Eye muscles |
| 7 | Facial | Face movement, taste, glandular function |
| 8 | Vestibulocochlear | Balance, hearing |
| 9 | Glossopharyngeal | Taste, ear/throat movement |
| 10 | Vagus | Ear sensation, digestion, heart/throat motor |
| 11 | Accessory | Head, neck, shoulder muscles |
| 12 | Hypoglossal | Tongue motor |
