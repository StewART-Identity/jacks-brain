The **central nervous system (CNS)** consists of the brain and the spinal cord that extends from it. Together they receive chemical and electrical signals from throughout the body, interpret those signals, and coordinate responses — controlling everything from voluntary movement to autonomic processes like breathing and heart rate.

## Components

### Brain

The brain is the CNS's command center. At roughly 3 pounds, it processes sensory input, generates thought and emotion, and issues motor commands. Its major structural divisions are covered in [[collection/concepts/brain-anatomy]].

### Spinal cord

The spinal cord extends downward from the base of the medulla (the lowest part of the brainstem) through a large opening in the skull base, running through the vertebral column. It carries messages bidirectionally between the brain and the body's peripheral nerves. In the spinal cord, [[collection/concepts/gray-matter-and-white-matter]] positions are reversed from the brain: white matter is on the outside, gray matter sits within.

## Neural signaling

The CNS relies on billions of **neurons** (nerve cells) to transmit information. Signals travel as both electrical impulses along axons and chemical signals across synapses. Some signals stay within the brain; others travel down the spinal cord and out through peripheral nerves to distant body parts.

## Protective structures

### Meninges

Three layers of membrane enclose both the brain and spinal cord:

1. **Dura mater** — tough outer layer
2. **Arachnoid mater** — thin weblike middle layer
3. **Pia mater** — thin inner membrane closely following the neural surface

### Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)

CSF is produced by the brain's four **ventricles** (interconnected chambers) and circulates continuously around the brain and spinal cord in the space below the arachnoid mater. Its functions:

- Physical cushioning of the brain and spinal cord
- Removal of metabolic waste and impurities
- Delivery of nutrients

The meninges and CSF together form a protective envelope around the entire CNS.

## Relationship to peripheral nervous system

The CNS is distinct from the peripheral nervous system (PNS), which comprises the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord. The twelve **cranial nerves** represent a partial exception — they originate in the CNS (brain and brainstem) but extend into the head, neck, and body, bridging the two systems. See [[collection/concepts/brain-anatomy]] for the full list of cranial nerves and their functions.
