An accessible walkthrough of every significant brain structure, pairing anatomical location with functional role. Progresses from the cortical surface inward — lobes → brainstem → deep diencephalic structures → limbic system. Particularly strong on cortical area specificity (naming primary motor, somatosensory, visual, and auditory cortices) and on [[reflect/concepts/dopamine-pathways]] and [[reflect/concepts/basal-ganglia]] as systems.

## Cerebral cortex

The outer surface of the cerebral hemispheres is divided by **sulci** (fissures) and **gyri** (ridges). The **longitudinal fissure** separates the brain into two hemispheres. Sulci and gyri result from folding the **cerebral cortex** — the outermost layer — to maximize surface area within the skull. See [[reflect/concepts/brain-anatomy]] for the full structural overview and [[reflect/concepts/gray-matter-and-white-matter]] for the tissue-level distinction.

The cortex contains:
- **Sensory processing areas** for each sensory modality
- **Motor areas** initiating voluntary movement
- **Association areas** integrating inputs from multiple regions to support higher-order cognition

## Lobes and primary cortical areas

### Frontal lobe

| Area | Location | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Motor cortex** | Posterior frontal | Initiates voluntary movement |
| **Primary motor cortex** | Bulk of motor cortex | Neurons here directly cause movement in specific head and body regions |
| **Prefrontal cortex** | Anterior to motor cortex | Decision-making, planning, impulse control, pursuing long-term goals |
| **Broca's area** | Left frontal (most people) | Language production; damage disrupts ability to produce speech |

### Parietal lobe

Separated from the frontal lobe by the **central sulcus**. Involved in language, spatial orientation, and attention. Contains the **primary somatosensory cortex** — processes touch, pain, temperature, and proprioception (awareness of body position in space).

### Occipital lobe

Located at the very back of the brain. Primarily visual processing. Contains the **primary visual cortex** plus several secondary visual areas.

### Temporal lobe

Handles higher-order visual processing, learning and memory, and hearing. Contains the **primary auditory cortex** (sound perception). Near the temporal-parietal junction sits **Wernicke's area**, associated with language comprehension; its precise contribution is still being investigated.

## Cerebellum

Extends from the back and bottom of the brain. Best known for movement-related roles:
- Motor coordination and control
- Balance and posture
- Fine-tuning of voluntary movements
- Movement-related learning

## Brainstem

Connects brain to spinal cord. Divided into three regions from lowest to highest:

### Medulla (medulla oblongata)
Lowest portion, just above the spinal cord. Regulates vital cardiovascular and respiratory functions.

### Pons
Carries information to and from the cerebellum. Houses nuclei associated with **cranial nerves** serving sensory and motor functions of the head and face.

### Midbrain
Uppermost brainstem region. Contains the two largest dopamine-producing areas in the brain — see [[reflect/concepts/dopamine-pathways]] for the full treatment:

- **Substantia nigra**: dopamine neurons here are critical for movement. Their loss is the hallmark of Parkinson's disease.
- **Ventral tegmental area (VTA)**: dopamine neurons project to the **nucleus accumbens**, forming the mesolimbic pathway central to reward and addiction.

Both the substantia nigra and nucleus accumbens are part of the [[reflect/concepts/basal-ganglia]].

## Thalamus

Situated above the brainstem. Often called a **relay station**: virtually all sensory information (except olfaction) passes through a specialized thalamic nucleus before being forwarded to the appropriate cortical area for further processing. See [[reflect/concepts/brain-anatomy]] for its structural context.

## Pineal gland

Posterior to the thalamus. Secretes **melatonin**, primarily during darkness, to regulate circadian rhythms.

## Hypothalamus

Just below the thalamus. Maintains homeostasis across many bodily systems by regulating:
- Thirst and drinking
- Hunger and feeding
- Body temperature
- Stress responses
- Circadian rhythms

The hypothalamus connects to the **pituitary gland** directly below it. The pituitary releases hormones (growth hormone, TSH, FSH, LH) in response to hypothalamic signals. It also releases **oxytocin** and **vasopressin**, which are synthesized in the hypothalamus and transported down for secretion.

## Hippocampus

Located in the temporal lobe (not visible from a lateral view). Especially important for converting short-term memories into long-term memories.

## Amygdala

Adjacent to the hippocampus; almond-shaped. Best known for processing fear and responding to environmental threats, but also involved in positive emotions including pleasure.

## Corpus callosum

C-shaped bundle of axons — the largest in the brain — connecting the left and right hemispheres and enabling communication between them.

## Ventricles and CSF

A system of interconnected cavities throughout the brain produces **cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)**. CSF functions:
- Surrounds and cushions the brain (reduces strain from gravity)
- Removes metabolic waste products
- Regulates the extracellular environment of neurons
