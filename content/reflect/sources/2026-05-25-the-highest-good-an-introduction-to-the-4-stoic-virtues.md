An introductory essay from Daily Stoic presenting the Stoic account of *summum bonum* — the highest good: virtue as the organizing principle of a well-lived life, expressed through four cardinal virtues (wisdom, courage, temperance, justice). The piece draws on [[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]], [[reflect/entities/epictetus]], [[reflect/entities/seneca]], and [[reflect/entities/cicero]] to construct a unified account of virtue as the only thing genuinely within our control, and therefore the only reliable foundation for flourishing.

## Summum Bonum

*Summum bonum* — Cicero's Latin for "the highest good" — is the organizing concept of Stoic ethics. The Stoics held that virtue is the only unconditional good: it is not contingent on external circumstances, luck, or social standing, and it is the one thing no misfortune can remove. Everything else — health, wealth, reputation, love — may follow from virtue, but virtue does not depend on any of them. Cicero: "The man who has virtue is in need of nothing whatever for the purpose of living well."

## The Four Virtues

### Wisdom

Diogenes Laërtius wrote that the Stoics defined wisdom as "the knowledge of things good and evil and of what is neither good nor evil… knowledge of what we ought to choose, what we ought to beware of, and what is indifferent." [[reflect/entities/epictetus]] sharpened the practical task: "The chief task in life is simply this: to identify and separate matters so that I can say clearly to myself which are externals not under my control, and which have to do with the choices I actually control."

Viktor Frankl's formulation captures the operational consequence: "Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response." Wisdom is the capacity to recognize that space and act from it rather than react through it. [[reflect/entities/seneca]] compressed the practical conclusion: "Works not words."

### Temperance

Temperance — the Stoics often used it interchangeably with "self-control" — is Aristotle's "golden mean": virtue found firmly between excess and deficiency. The Stoics extended it beyond material goods to discipline in all conditions: pleasure or pain, admiration or contempt, failure or triumph.

[[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]]'s working definition (Meditations 4.24): "Do less. Or (more accurately) do what's essential — what the logos of a social being requires, and in the requisite way." Ask at every moment: "Is this necessary?" The insight is that abundance comes from having what is essential, not from accumulation. Epictetus: "Curb your desire — don't set your heart on so many things and you will get what you need." Seneca: "You ask what is the proper limit to a person's wealth? First, having what is essential, and second, having what is enough."

### Courage

The Stoic account of courage is not heroic performance — it is the willingness to face what must be faced, to resist the comfort of the status quo, and to hold to principles when doing so is costly and the outcome is uncertain. Epictetus' prescription: two words — "persist and resist."

The essay illustrates courage through historical cases:
- **Thrasea** challenged Nero's tyranny knowing it would cost his life and would fail to stop the man
- **The Percy family** — LeRoy fighting the Klan in 1922, William Alexander giving up bachelorhood to adopt his young cousins, Walker Percy resisting the racism of his generation through writing
- **Publius Rutilius Rufus** facing false accusations and unjust prosecution to ultimately inspire reform against corruption
- **Marcus Aurelius** himself: the lifetime struggle not to be corrupted by absolute power

Seneca's last words to Nero: "Nero can kill me, but he cannot harm me." The distinction is characteristically Stoic: death is an external; virtue cannot be taken away.

### Justice

Of the four virtues, Marcus Aurelius called justice "the source of all the other virtues" and the most important. [[reflect/entities/cicero]] agreed: justice is "the crowning glory of the virtues" and "the principle which constitutes the bond of human society and of a virtual community of life." From his *De Officiis*, justice requires:

- Do no harm to another
- Use common possessions as common; private as belonging to their owners
- We are not born for ourselves alone
- Men were brought into being for the sake of men, that they might do good to one another
- Contribute to the common good
- Maintain good faith, steadfastness, and truth

Behind the justice virtue is [[reflect/concepts/sympatheia]] — the Stoic belief in mutual interdependence among everything in the universe. Marcus Aurelius: "What injures the hive injures the bee." Epictetus: "Seeking the very best in ourselves means actively caring for the welfare of other human beings." Robert Greene: "We are all the same. The Stoics talk about that. It's logos. It's what unites everything together."

## The unifying argument

The essay's central claim: [[reflect/concepts/stoicism]] is not grandiose or vague but precisely practical. "A Stoic believes they don't control the world around them, only how they respond — and that they must always respond with courage, temperance, wisdom, and justice." The four virtues map the response space: wisdom identifies the controllable; temperance disciplines the response to avoid excess; courage enables action despite cost or uncertainty; justice orients the action toward the common good rather than self-interest.

The essay closes with Marcus Aurelius's test of the framework's adequacy: if you come across anything better than justice, prudence, self-control, and courage — embrace it. He concluded nothing met that bar.
