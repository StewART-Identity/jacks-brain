Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) was a Greek Stoic philosopher born into slavery in Hierapolis (modern Turkey). After being freed, he established a school of philosophy in Nicopolis. His teachings were recorded by his student Arrian in the *Discourses* and condensed in the *Enchiridion* (Handbook) — texts that form the practical core of Stoic philosophy and remain in continuous use.

## The dichotomy of control

His central teaching is the "dichotomy of control": the distinction between what is "up to us" (*eph' hēmin*) — our judgments, desires, responses, and choices — and what is not: external events, other people's actions, bodily states, reputation. The entire Stoic practice follows from getting this distinction right and focusing energy exclusively on the former.

His formulation of wisdom: "The chief task in life is simply this: to identify and separate matters so that I can say clearly to myself which are externals not under my control, and which have to do with the choices I actually control. Where then do I look for good and evil? Not to uncontrollable externals, but within myself to the choices that are my own."

## The four virtues, as expressed by Epictetus

- **Wisdom**: recognize the space between stimulus and response; act from judgment rather than reaction
- **Temperance**: "Curb your desire — don't set your heart on so many things and you will get what you need"
- **Courage**: two words — "persist and resist"
- **Justice**: "Seeking the very best in ourselves means actively caring for the welfare of other human beings"

## Connection to clinical psychology

Psychotherapist and historian Donald Robertson (referenced in [[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-the-cognitive-peak-that-will-never-happen-again]]) argues that Epictetus functions in clinical literature "almost as a patron saint of resilience research." The foundations of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — one of the most rigorously validated therapeutic approaches in experimental psychology — map almost directly onto Epictetus' practice. This convergence across two millennia and independent epistemic routes is significant: see [[reflect/synthesis/stoic-virtue-prescription-and-discovery]].

## Historical significance

Epictetus is notable as a Stoic philosopher who came from outside the elite. Unlike [[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]] (emperor) or [[reflect/entities/seneca]] (senator's advisor) or [[reflect/entities/cicero]] (senator), Epictetus was enslaved for much of his life. His authority derived entirely from the quality of his thought and practice, not from social position — which is consistent with the Stoic insistence that virtue, not circumstance, is the source of genuine good.
