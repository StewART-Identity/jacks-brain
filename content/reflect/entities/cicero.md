Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) was a Roman statesman, orator, lawyer, and philosopher — Rome's greatest orator and, as John Adams described him, a figure across history without peer as a combined statesman and philosopher. Thomas Jefferson identified his works among the "elementary books of public right" underlying the Declaration of Independence.

Cicero held every important Roman office by the youngest legally allowable ages. His philosophical writing synthesized Greek Stoic thought for Latin audiences and introduced the term *summum bonum* — the highest good — as a philosophical organizing concept.

## De Officiis and the four virtues

In *De Officiis* (On Moral Duties), Cicero presented the four Stoic virtues as a unified system and placed them in a hierarchical relationship: justice is "the crowning glory of the virtues." He agreed with [[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]] that justice is the most important — the principle that gives the others their meaning and connects them to the social world.

His definition of justice: "the principle which constitutes the bond of human society and of a virtual community of life." Its practical content:

- Do no harm to another
- Use common possessions as common; private as belonging to their owners
- We are not born for ourselves alone
- Men were brought into being for the sake of men, that they might do good to one another
- Contribute to the common good
- Maintain good faith, steadfastness, and truth

Unjust action, for Cicero, is anything that inflicts injury or harm on another — typically done in pursuit of self-interest. The diagnostic question for evaluating any action: does it harm someone?

## Connection to sympatheia

Cicero's justice is grounded in the Stoic concept of [[reflect/concepts/sympatheia]] — the doctrine that all persons are parts of one interconnected whole, and that harming any part is therefore irrational as well as wrong. The *summum bonum* is not a personal achievement but a condition of the interconnected whole: virtue expressed in right relationship with others.

## Relationship to other Stoics

Cicero predated [[reflect/entities/epictetus]], [[reflect/entities/seneca]], and [[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]] by roughly a century. He was the primary conduit through which Greek Stoic philosophy reached Roman (and later European) intellectual culture.
