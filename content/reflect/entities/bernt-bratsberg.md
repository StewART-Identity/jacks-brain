Bernt Bratsberg is a Norwegian economist affiliated with the Ragnar Frisch Centre for Economic Research in Oslo. He is known in the cognitive development literature primarily for a 2018 study co-authored with Ole Rogeberg, published in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* (PNAS).

## The 2018 PNAS study

The paper analyzed cognitive ability test scores from over 730,000 Norwegian male military conscripts born between 1962 and 1991 — a sample large enough to detect small but real generational trends with high statistical confidence. Key findings:

- IQ scores peaked in cohorts born around **1975** — the middle of Generation X
- Scores have declined at approximately **0.2 IQ points per birth cohort per year** since that peak
- Critically, the analysis compared **brothers within the same household** — holding genetics and family environment constant. The decline persisted even within families, ruling out genetic explanations (dysgenics, immigration demographics, etc.)

The within-family design is the methodological contribution that gave the study its weight: if you hold the family constant and the decline still appears, the cause must be in the *changing environment between older and younger siblings' childhoods*, not in who was having children. Something environmental changed after the mid-1970s developmental window.

## Significance

The study established the empirical foundation for the argument in [[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-the-cognitive-peak-that-will-never-happen-again]]: that whatever produced the [[reflect/concepts/flynn-effect]] reversal was environmental rather than genetic, and that it affected cohorts born after roughly 1975. The specific environmental candidates — lead removal, screen adoption, loss of unstructured time — are discussed in that source.
