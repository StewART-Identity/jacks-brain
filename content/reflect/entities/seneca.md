Lucius Annaeus Seneca (c. 4 BCE–65 CE), known as Seneca the Younger, was a Roman Stoic philosopher, playwright, and statesman who served as an advisor to Emperor Nero. After falling out of favor with Nero, he was ordered to commit suicide. Contemporary accounts describe his death as calm and deliberate — conducted in the manner his philosophy prescribed.

## Key positions

Seneca's writing is compact and aphoristic — practically oriented toward the question of how to live well under conditions one cannot control. His best-known works are the *Letters to Lucilius* (*Epistulae Morales*) and the *Moral Essays*.

On **wisdom**: "Works not words." Philosophical study that doesn't change behavior is decoration, not practice.

On **temperance**: "You ask what is the proper limit to a person's wealth? First, having what is essential, and second, having what is enough." Abundance is not accumulation; it is sufficiency.

On **courage**: His final recorded words, as Nero's order arrived, were addressed to the emperor's messengers: "Nero can kill me, but he cannot harm me." The distinction is characteristically Stoic: death is an external — outside one's control and therefore not a genuine harm; virtue and judgment cannot be taken away. This is the application of the dichotomy of control to the most extreme possible circumstance.

## Relationship to other Stoics

Seneca was a generation older than [[reflect/entities/epictetus]] and preceded [[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]] by a century. His work forms one of the three primary documentary pillars of practical Stoicism, alongside the *Discourses* of Epictetus and the *Meditations* of Marcus Aurelius.

[[reflect/entities/cicero]] predated all three and was influential in transmitting Greek Stoic thought to Roman audiences and defining the four-virtue framework in *De Officiis*.
