Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) was Roman Emperor from 161 to 180 CE and a practicing Stoic philosopher. His private journals — known as the *Meditations* — were written in Greek as personal reminders of Stoic discipline, never intended for publication, and became one of the most read and influential philosophical texts from antiquity.

## Philosophy

Of the four Stoic virtues (wisdom, courage, temperance, justice), Marcus Aurelius considered justice "the source of all the other virtues" and the most important — the principle that gives the others their meaning. Without justice, courage is merely self-serving, and wisdom is wasted.

His characteristic voice in the *Meditations* is self-directed: not instruction to others but reminders to himself. This is consistent with the Stoic practice of *premeditatio* (premeditation of difficult scenarios) and ongoing self-examination. Selected examples:

- On temperance: "If you seek tranquillity, do less. Or (more accurately) do what's essential. Ask yourself at every moment, 'Is this necessary?'" (4.24)
- On justice: "A commitment to justice in your own acts. Which means: thought and action resulting in the common good. What you were born to do." (9.31)
- On [[reflect/concepts/sympatheia]]: "What injures the hive injures the bee."
- On the virtue test: "If you should come across anything better than justice, prudence, self-control, courage — embrace it without reservations. If nothing presents itself superior to these, don't make room for anything but it."

## As exemplar of Stoic courage

[[reflect/sources/2026-05-25-the-highest-good-an-introduction-to-the-4-stoic-virtues]] presents Marcus Aurelius as an exemplar of Stoic courage in a particular sense: not battlefield heroism but the lifetime internal struggle to remain a good person while wielding absolute power amid Rome's decadence and decline. This was a war against himself, conducted within himself, for the entirety of his reign.
