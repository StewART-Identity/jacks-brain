Gray matter and white matter are the two primary tissue types of the [[reflect/concepts/central-nervous-system]]. Their distinct compositions give them different functional roles and different appearances on imaging scans.

## Composition

| | Gray matter | White matter |
|-|-------------|--------------|
| **Primary component** | Neuron somas (cell bodies) | Axons wrapped in myelin |
| **Function** | Processing and interpreting information | Transmitting information to other CNS regions |
| **Appearance** | Darker | Lighter |

### Neurons

A **neuron** has two key parts relevant here:

- **Soma (cell body)**: the round central body containing the nucleus. Clusters of somas form gray matter.
- **Axon**: the long projecting stem that carries signals away from the soma toward other neurons or muscle cells. Axons are bundled together and wrapped in myelin to form white matter tracts.

### Myelin

**Myelin** is a fatty protective sheath produced by [[reflect/concepts/neuroglial-cells|glial cells]] (Schwann cells in the periphery; oligodendrocytes in the CNS) that wraps around axons. Myelin dramatically speeds up signal transmission by enabling **saltatory conduction** — the electrical impulse jumps between gaps in the myelin (nodes of Ranvier) rather than traveling continuously along the entire axon surface. Demyelinating diseases (such as multiple sclerosis) degrade this speed advantage.

## Spatial arrangement

In the **brain**, gray matter forms the outer layer (the cerebral cortex), with white matter in the inner core. In the **spinal cord**, this arrangement is reversed: white matter forms the outer columns, and gray matter sits in the butterfly-shaped interior.

## Functional consequence

Because gray matter is where neurons integrate inputs and compute outputs, it is the seat of higher cognitive functions — perception, memory, decision-making, language. White matter is the brain's communication infrastructure, enabling distant gray-matter regions to coordinate rapidly. Damage to white matter (e.g., in strokes or trauma) disrupts connectivity between regions without destroying the processing centers themselves; damage to gray matter typically reduces processing capacity directly.
