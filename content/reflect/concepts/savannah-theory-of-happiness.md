The Savannah Theory of Happiness, proposed by evolutionary psychologists [[reflect/entities/satoshi-kanazawa]] (London School of Economics) and Norman Li (Singapore Management University) in a 2016 paper, argues that human happiness preferences reflect evolutionary mismatch — the brain evolved to respond to an ancestral environment of small hunter-gatherer bands of roughly 150 people, not to contemporary conditions.

## The baseline finding

For **average-intelligence people**, the theory's predictions match baseline research findings:

- Regular contact with close friends increases life satisfaction
- High population density (crowds, strangers) decreases life satisfaction

This is consistent with the brain having been optimized for small-band living, where a dense social network of familiar people was the norm and high population density represented unfamiliar social terrain.

## The high-IQ reversal

The unexpected finding from Kanazawa and Li's analysis of 15,000+ adults: for **high-IQ individuals**, the effect of frequent social interaction on life satisfaction was **negative** — not merely neutral, but negatively correlated. More time with friends, more socializing, lower reported life satisfaction. Statistically significant.

The proposed mechanism: general intelligence evolved specifically as an adaptation for solving novel problems that ancestors had not previously encountered — situations requiring improvisation rather than learned scripts. Individuals with higher intelligence are better equipped to navigate novel environments independently; they need the tribal safety net *less*. When highly intelligent people spend time alone, they are doing what their cognitive architecture is specifically optimized for: engaging with novel problems independently. High-frequency surface socializing interrupts that process without compensating.

## Implications for Gen X

[[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-the-cognitive-peak-that-will-never-happen-again]] applies the Savannah Theory to explain what researchers had previously framed as pathology in Gen X men — preference for solitude, small social circles, exhaustion at large gatherings they have difficulty articulating. The theory reframes this: for a mind operating at the upper end of the distribution, these are *evolutionary signatures*, not disorders. Not trauma, not social anxiety, not something requiring repair.

The quality-to-quantity trade-off the theory implies: one substantive conversation with a person who thinks carefully is worth a great deal; three hours at a party managing surface-level exchanges is a net cognitive cost.

## Relationship to the solitude/isolation distinction

The Savannah Theory explains the *preference* for solitude but does not endorse isolation. [[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-the-cognitive-peak-that-will-never-happen-again]] draws the distinction explicitly: solitude gives energy back and still allows connection; isolation slowly depletes and starts treating connection as an interruption. The theory legitimizes solitude as an adaptive state for high-IQ minds; it does not legitimize using it to rationalize the gradual elimination of all meaningful connection. A precise mind can build the most convincing argument for the wrong side of that line.
