The basal ganglia are a collection of subcortical nuclei (clusters of neurons) that work together to regulate movement, habit formation, and reward processing. They are not a single structure but a functionally linked group distributed across the forebrain and midbrain.

## Key components

| Structure | Location | Role |
|-----------|----------|------|
| **Substantia nigra** | Midbrain | Dopamine production for movement (nigrostriatal pathway) |
| **Nucleus accumbens** | Forebrain | Reward processing (mesolimbic pathway) |
| **Striatum** | Forebrain | Primary input station of the basal ganglia |
| **Globus pallidus** | Forebrain | Output relay modulating thalamus and cortex |
| **Subthalamic nucleus** | Diencephalon | Modulates movement excitation/inhibition balance |

## Functions

**Movement**: The basal ganglia select and suppress motor programs — they help initiate intended movements and suppress competing ones. Dysfunction produces movement disorders: too little basal ganglia activity leads to difficulty initiating movement (as in Parkinson's); too much activity in certain circuits can produce involuntary movements (as in Huntington's disease).

**Habit formation**: The basal ganglia are essential for consolidating repeated behaviors into automatized habits. Stimulus-response associations learned over time are encoded in basal ganglia circuitry, which is why habitual behaviors feel effortless and are difficult to extinguish.

**Reward processing**: The nucleus accumbens in particular acts as a convergence point for dopamine signals from the [[reflect/concepts/dopamine-pathways#mesolimbic-pathway]], cortical input, and limbic input. This convergence makes the nucleus accumbens a hub for evaluating the rewarding value of actions and stimuli.

## Disease relevance

- **Parkinson's disease**: selective loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra disrupts the nigrostriatal pathway, impairing the basal ganglia's ability to facilitate movement.
- **Addiction**: addictive substances abnormally amplify dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens, hijacking the basal ganglia's reward and habit circuits.
