**Neuroglial cells** (or simply **glia**) are the non-neuronal support cells of the nervous system. They do not generate or conduct action potentials but perform essential structural, metabolic, and protective functions that neurons depend on. Neuroglial cells are divided by location: four types reside in the [[reflect/concepts/central-nervous-system]] (CNS), and two types in the peripheral nervous system (PNS). See [[reflect/concepts/gray-matter-and-white-matter]] for how myelinating glia determine white matter's structure and function.

## CNS neuroglial cells

### Astrocytes

Astrocytes are the most abundant glial cell in the CNS. Their primary roles:

- **Blood-brain barrier maintenance**: astrocytes wrap around the capillaries supplying the brain, helping regulate what crosses from blood into neural tissue. This barrier is the CNS's first line of immune defense.
- **Metabolic support**: they supply neurons with nutrients, regulate extracellular ion concentrations, and remove neurotransmitters from synapses (synaptic recycling).
- **Structural support**: their star-shaped ("astro") morphology lets them bridge neurons and blood vessels, supporting both.

### Ependymal cells

Ependymal cells line the **ventricles** of the brain and the central canal of the spinal cord. They are involved in producing, circulating, and monitoring **cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)** — the clear fluid that cushions the CNS, clears metabolic waste, and delivers nutrients. See [[reflect/concepts/central-nervous-system]] for CSF's role in the CNS protective system.

### Oligodendrocytes

Oligodendrocytes are the **myelinating glial cells of the CNS**. Each oligodendrocyte extends processes that wrap around multiple axon segments, forming the **myelin sheath** — a fatty insulating layer that enables rapid saltatory conduction of nerve signals. Oligodendrocytes also provide structural support to CNS tissue. Their PNS counterpart is the Schwann cell (below). Demyelinating diseases such as **multiple sclerosis** involve the destruction of oligodendrocytes and their myelin sheaths.

### Microglia

Microglia are the **resident immune cells of the CNS**. They perform:

- **Phagocytosis**: engulfing and destroying dead cells, cellular debris, and pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi)
- **Immune surveillance**: continuously scanning the neural environment for damage or infection
- **Synaptic pruning**: during development, eliminating excess synaptic connections

Because the blood-brain barrier (maintained by astrocytes) limits peripheral immune cell access, microglia are the CNS's primary internal defense mechanism.

## PNS neuroglial cells

### Satellite cells

Satellite cells surround the **cell bodies (somas) of neurons** in the ganglia of the peripheral nervous system. Their roles:

- Structurally support and insulate neuron cell bodies
- Regulate the chemical environment around neuronal somas
- Help control **neurotransmitter levels** in the ganglionic microenvironment

### Schwann cells

Schwann cells are the **myelinating glial cells of the PNS** — the peripheral counterpart to oligodendrocytes. Key distinctions:

| | Oligodendrocytes | Schwann cells |
|--|--|--|
| **Location** | CNS | PNS |
| **Myelination** | One cell myelinates *multiple* axon segments | One cell myelinates a *single* axon segment |
| **Regeneration** | Limited CNS regenerative support | Active: guide and support axon regrowth after injury |

Schwann cells support **nerve regeneration** after peripheral nerve injury by forming a conduit that guides regrowth of damaged axons — a capacity oligodendrocytes lack, which is part of why CNS injuries are generally harder to recover from than PNS injuries.

## CNS vs. PNS support architecture

A notable pattern: the CNS and PNS evolved parallel glial support systems performing analogous functions with different cell types:

| Function | CNS cell | PNS cell |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Myelination | Oligodendrocytes | Schwann cells |
| Soma support / environment regulation | Astrocytes | Satellite cells |
| Immune defense | Microglia | (peripheral immune cells) |
| CSF / fluid boundary | Ependymal cells | — |

The CNS invests more heavily in immune isolation (astrocyte barrier, microglia) while the PNS prioritizes regenerative capacity (Schwann cell-guided regrowth). This asymmetry partially explains the greater fragility of CNS injuries versus the greater recovery potential of peripheral nerve injuries.
