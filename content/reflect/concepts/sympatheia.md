*Sympatheia* (from Greek: συμπάθεια, "fellow-feeling") is the Stoic doctrine of universal interdependence — the belief that all persons, beings, and things in the universe are parts of a single interconnected whole governed by *logos* (the rational principle that runs through all things). Because all persons participate in this common rational nature, the welfare of one is inseparable from the welfare of all.

The Stoics called it the most radical idea in their system. [[reflect/sources/2026-05-25-the-highest-good-an-introduction-to-the-4-stoic-virtues]] presents it as such.

## Primary expressions

[[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]], most compactly: "What injures the hive injures the bee."

[[reflect/entities/epictetus]]: "Seeking the very best in ourselves means actively caring for the welfare of other human beings."

Musonius Rufus (Epictetus' teacher): "To honor equality, to want to do good, and for a person, being human, to not want to harm human beings — this is the most honorable lesson and it makes just people out of those who learn it."

Robert Greene: "We are all the same. The Stoics talk about that. It's logos. It's what unites everything together."

## Relationship to justice

Sympatheia is the philosophical grounding for the Stoic virtue of justice. If all persons are genuinely one interconnected whole, then harming another is a form of self-harm, and acting for the common good is not merely altruistic but rational. [[reflect/entities/cicero]] defined justice as "the principle which constitutes the bond of human society and of a virtual community of life" — sympatheia provides the metaphysical basis for why that bond exists and why it matters.

## Relationship to courage

[[reflect/sources/2026-05-25-the-highest-good-an-introduction-to-the-4-stoic-virtues]] raises the diagnostic question: "How impressive is courage if it's only about self-interest?" Sympatheia is what prevents the Stoic virtues from collapsing into sophisticated egoism. Wisdom, temperance, and courage earn their place in the virtue framework only insofar as they are oriented toward the common good, not merely the self. Sympatheia is the principle that makes this orientation non-optional.

## Practical obligations

From sympatheia, [[reflect/entities/cicero]] derived specific practical obligations: do no harm, do not appropriate common goods, act as if we were not born for ourselves alone. [[reflect/entities/marcus-aurelius]] expressed the action consequence directly: "thought and action resulting in the common good. What you were born to do." (Meditations 9.31)

## Contrast with self-interest ethics

Sympatheia is explicitly a rejection of ethical frameworks grounded in self-interest. The Stoics held that the Epicurean pursuit of personal pleasure, or any other form of enlightened self-interest, missed the point: because the self is not genuinely separable from the whole, building one's ethics on an isolated self is building on a fiction.
