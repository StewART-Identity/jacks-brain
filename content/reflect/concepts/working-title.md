A **working title** is an informal title that describes the role a person functionally performs — or aspires to perform — before the organization has formally assigned that title in its HR or employee-information system. Working titles emerge when role responsibilities expand faster than institutional processes can ratify the change.

## Characteristics

- **Functionally accurate, institutionally unrecognized.** The person is doing the work the title describes, but the formal record (payroll system, org chart, EIS) carries a different title.
- **Conditionally used.** Organizations may tolerate informal use in certain contexts (internal discussions, meeting introductions) while restricting it in formal ones (signed documents, official signatures, published materials).
- **No guaranteed path to formalization.** A title being "actively pursued" or "on the radar" does not imply a timeline. The working title can persist indefinitely without ever becoming the formal title.

## Risks and friction

Using a working title on formal materials creates several problems:
- External audiences treat the title as authoritative, causing confusion when the formal title diverges
- Internal questions arise about scope, authority, and compensation expectations
- If formalization never arrives, the gap between expectation and reality becomes a source of workplace tension

## Primary example in this wiki

In April 2026, [[reflect/entities/ryan-kane]] instructed Jack Stewart to stop using "IAM Architect" on formal documents and signatures, noting there was no timeline for that title to be formalized in UNT System's EIS. Jack had been using the title because he believed he was functioning as the "working" architect for the Entra project and the broader IAM team. The conversation is documented in [[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-pasted-image-1779669099737]].

This episode is the basis for the wiki's normalization rule: Jack's title is **IAM Engineer** — the title EIS carries — rather than "IAM Architect" or any variant.
