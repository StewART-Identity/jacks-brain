Dopamine is a neurotransmitter produced primarily in two midbrain regions. From there, dopamine neurons project through distinct circuits to different brain targets, with markedly different functional consequences. Both source regions sit within the [[reflect/concepts/basal-ganglia]].

## The two major pathways

### Nigrostriatal pathway

**Origin**: **Substantia nigra** (midbrain)  
**Target**: Striatum (a component of the basal ganglia)  
**Function**: Movement control — dopamine here is required for smooth, coordinated voluntary movement.

When substantia nigra dopamine neurons die in large numbers, the result is **Parkinson's disease**: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (slowness), and difficulty initiating movement. Parkinson's is therefore in part a disease of dopamine depletion in the nigrostriatal circuit.

### Mesolimbic pathway

**Origin**: **Ventral tegmental area (VTA)** (midbrain)  
**Target**: **Nucleus accumbens** (and other limbic/prefrontal areas)  
**Function**: Reward processing, motivation, and reinforcement learning.

The VTA-to-nucleus-accumbens projection is critically important for rewarding experiences. Addictive drugs co-opt this pathway — most drugs of abuse either mimic dopamine, block its reuptake, or otherwise amplify dopamine signaling in the nucleus accumbens, producing an abnormally strong reward signal that drives compulsive use.

## Why the distinction matters

The two pathways can be affected independently:
- Parkinson's disease selectively targets the nigrostriatal pathway without initially disrupting mesolimbic reward circuits.
- Antipsychotic drugs that broadly block dopamine receptors may reduce psychotic symptoms (which involve mesolimbic overactivity) while also causing movement side-effects by dampening the nigrostriatal pathway.

## Structural context

Both the substantia nigra and nucleus accumbens are components of the [[reflect/concepts/basal-ganglia]], a group of deep brain structures also involved in habit formation.
