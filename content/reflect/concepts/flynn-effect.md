The Flynn Effect refers to the decades-long trend of rising average IQ test scores observed across much of the 20th century in industrialized nations. Named after researcher James Flynn, who systematically documented the phenomenon. The cause was long debated — better nutrition, expanded education, increased test familiarity, and environmental improvements were all proposed as contributing factors.

## The reversal and its peak

[[reflect/entities/bernt-bratsberg]] and Ole Rogeberg's 2018 paper in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* analyzed cognitive test data from over 730,000 Norwegian male military conscripts born between 1962 and 1991 — a sample large enough to detect small generational trends with high confidence. Key findings:

- Scores reached their absolute historical peak in cohorts born around **1975** — the middle of Generation X
- Scores began declining after the mid-1970s cohort at approximately **0.2 IQ points per birth cohort per year**
- Compounded across 30 years of birth cohorts, this represents a meaningful, measurable cognitive gap between the peak generation and those that followed

A 2023 Northwestern University study confirmed the American side: verbal reasoning, matrix reasoning, and numerical series all declined for post-1975 cohorts. The only domain that improved was 3D rotation — likely from video games. See [[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-the-cognitive-peak-that-will-never-happen-again]].

## The critical methodological finding

Bratsberg and Rogeberg's study compared **brothers within the same household** — holding genetics, family environment, and socioeconomic status constant. The decline still appeared: the younger brother consistently scored lower than the older brother born a few years earlier. This ruled out:

- Genetic explanations (same family, same genes)
- Immigration demographics or dysgenic explanations
- Family-level socioeconomic factors

If the pattern holds within families, the cause must be in the **environment that changed** between older and younger siblings' developmental windows — not in who was having children.

## Environmental explanation: the cognitive Goldilocks zone

[[reflect/sources/2026-05-24-the-cognitive-peak-that-will-never-happen-again]] proposes that the 1975 peak reflects a unique convergence of environmental improvements reaching their optimal ceiling simultaneously:

- **Lead removal**: leaded gasoline was phased out 1970 to the mid-1980s, eliminating a neurotoxin estimated to have suppressed prior cohorts' IQ by 4–5 points. This environmental upgrade landed precisely on Gen X's developmental window.
- **Nutritional improvements and expanded education** reaching optimal contribution at the same moment
- **Pre-screen childhood**: the critical factor explaining why millennials — born into an even lower-lead environment — did not score higher. Screens arrived and replaced the effortful cognitive demands that had been building neural architecture

The "Goldilocks" framing: all positive environmental factors had optimized, and the cognitive-demand-reducing effects of screens had not yet arrived.

## Relationship to cognitive reserve

The Flynn Effect reversal is consistent with the [[reflect/concepts/cognitive-reserve]] hypothesis: that capacity for complex reasoning is built by effortful, unsupervised problem-solving during childhood. [[reflect/concepts/latchkey-generation]] childhoods provided this demand; screens offered an alternative that satisfied the surface sensation of engagement without the cognitive work. Each cohort building brains in an environment with more ready answers and less unsupervised problem-solving may be building less cognitive reserve — showing up as declining scores in fluid reasoning, verbal reasoning, and abstract reasoning.
