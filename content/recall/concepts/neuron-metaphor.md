The use of neuron anatomy as a structural metaphor for how knowledge is received, integrated, and transmitted — the organizing principle behind Jack's Brain.

## The Mapping

| Neuron component | Wiki analogue |
|---|---|
| Dendrites | Ingested sources — many inputs from the outside world |
| Soma (cell body) | Entity and concept pages — integration and synthesis |
| Axon | Wikilinks — directed transmission to connected nodes |
| Synaptic terminals | Cross-references and synthesis pages — where knowledge acts on other knowledge |

## Why It Matters

Traditional document filing is hierarchical: one folder, one location. A neuron is not hierarchical — it is a *network node*. Every input arrives via dendrites from multiple directions; every output fans out to multiple targets. This matches the wiki's cross-referencing philosophy, where a single concept like [[recall/concepts/conditional-access-policy]] can be reached from a policy page, an entity page, a source summary, and a synthesis page simultaneously.

The metaphor also encodes a process model:

1. **Reception** — A source is ingested (dendrite fires).
2. **Integration** — The soma processes the signal: entities and concepts are updated, contradictions flagged.
3. **Transmission** — Wikilinks carry the integrated understanding outward to related pages.

## Biological Fidelity

The metaphor is loose by design. Biological neurons don't store knowledge — they participate in patterns across networks. The wiki takes liberties: individual pages do store content. But the key insight borrowed from neuroscience is that *connections are as important as nodes*. A page with no wikilinks is an isolated neuron that never fires — technically alive, functionally silent.
